Snowdonia National Park

Situated in North West Wales, almost 4 million people from around the globe visit the Snowdonia mountain range each year. With 1,497 miles of mountains, hills, lakes, coastline, beaches and history over an area of 823 square miles to explore it promises an adventure like no other you've ever been on. The highest peak stretches 3,560 feet high and is the tallest in both Wales and England. Named Eryri in Welsh, it is believed to have been populated by people since around 4000 to 3000 BCE.

Caernarfon Castle

Caernarfon castle is one of the most visited attractions in the whole of North Wales and one of the more interesting historical monuments throughout the whole UK. The castle as it stands today began construction in 1283 by King Edward I and he was the first of many royals to spend time at Caernarfon castle over its near 1000 year history and the castle today is a fascinating, storied structure to behold. Caernarfon castle was the location of the investiture of Charles, Prince of Wales in 1969, cementing the castle's continued importance to the Royal Family. The castle today is maintained and administered by the Welsh historic environment service, Cadw, who keep it safe for visitors to freely explore the outside and inside of the castle.

Historical Sites of North Wales

North Wales is a region of the world cloaked in history and will interest anyone looking for how people lived together, worked together and grew together with clear evidence left behind for us to find, investigate and wonder. Every relic is a monument to the unique Welsh history. From the prestige of the world's first modern suspension bridge in Menai Bridge to the industrial significance of the copper mines in Parys Mountain, North Wales has something for everyone.
